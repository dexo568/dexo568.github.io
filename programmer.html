Aside from my games, I've programmed a variety of other things, some of which you can see on my Github repository (including the source code for this very website!). They comprise a mixture of work for employers, labs that are a part of the curriculm at Washington University in St. Louis, where I am currently studying to receive my BS in Computer Science, and personal projects.

Most recently, I worked at Less Annoying Software as both a web developer and as customer support. Interacting with end-users was a first-time experience for me, and it was definitely an eye-opening one. Customers used the site in a way that was profoundly different than how I used it, and this revelation made me reconsider the way I tend to design UIs. I also spent a lot of time working on APIs, in particular integration with Zapier. This also required some thought on user-facing design, as the API endpoint data formed the basis of the Zapier UI. Previously, I had mostly worked on back-end or internal tools, so I enjoyed the opportunity to grow and learn more about front-end design.

Probably the most interesting project I've ever worked on is the NASA SOFIA project, which is an airborne observatory aboard a modified Boeing 747. During my time working on SOFIA (Stratospheric Observatory for Infrared Astronomy), I wrote the interface to one of the SOFIA instruments, the Echelon-Cross-Echelle Spectrograph (EXES), and also got to spend time working with the spectograph's optical systems. Although I can't pretend to completely understand the science behind ecehlon-echelle spectroscopy, as I'm a computer scientist, not a physicist, it was certainly a fascinating project and I feel lucky to have been able to contribute the interface. You can check out an interesting overview video of the project here.
